The Other Mind:
How Language Models Exhibit Human Temporal Cognition

Lingyu Li1,2 Yang Yao3 Yixu Wang1 Chunbo Li2 Yan Teng1† Yingchun Wang1
1Shanghai Artificial Intelligence Laboratory 2Shanghai Jiao Tong University 3The University of Hong Kong

TL;DR

Large language models spontaneously develop a subjective temporal reference point (~2025) and compress perceived time logarithmically — mirroring the human Weber-Fechner law — through a multi-level convergence at the neuronal, representational, and informational levels.

Figure 1 · Motivation

An experientialist perspective of LLMs' human-like cognition as a subjective construction of a shared external world by a convergent internal representational system. Both humans and LLMs are situated in overlapping information environments; their architectural differences lead to distinct yet sometimes convergent cognitive outcomes.

Abstract

As Large Language Models (LLMs) continue to advance, they exhibit certain cognitive patterns similar to those of humans that are not directly specified in training data. This study investigates this phenomenon by focusing on temporal cognition in LLMs. Leveraging the similarity judgment task, we find that larger models spontaneously establish a subjective temporal reference point and adhere to the Weber-Fechner law, whereby the perceived distance logarithmically compresses as years recede from this reference point.

To uncover the mechanisms behind this behavior, we conducted multiple analyses across neuronal, representational, and informational levels. We first identify a set of temporal-preferential neurons that exhibit minimal activation at the subjective reference point and implement a logarithmic coding scheme convergently found in biological systems. Probing representations of years reveals a hierarchical construction process, where years evolve from basic numerical values in shallow layers to abstract temporal orientation in deep layers. Finally, using pre-trained embedding models, we found that the training corpus itself possesses an inherent, non-linear temporal structure.

In discussion, we propose an experientialist perspective for understanding these findings, where LLMs' cognition is viewed as a subjective construction of the external world by its internal representational system. This nuanced perspective implies the potential emergence of alien cognitive frameworks that humans cannot intuitively predict, pointing toward a direction for AI alignment that focuses on guiding internal constructions.

Key Findings

01

Subjective Temporal Reference

Larger LLMs spontaneously establish a temporal reference point near the present (~2025) and perceive time logarithmically outward from it — identical to the Weber-Fechner law in human psychophysics.

02

Temporal-Preferential Neurons

A small subpopulation (0.67–1.71%) of FFN neurons concentrated in middle-to-late layers specifically encodes temporal information with logarithmic activation curves bottoming at the reference year.

03

Hierarchical Representation

Shallow layers encode raw numerical distance; deep layers reconstruct a reference-centered abstract temporal structure — a developmental trajectory sharpening with model scale.

04

Informational Pre-structure

The training corpus itself carries a non-linear temporal structure: years cluster densely in the distant past and future, providing raw material for the model's emergent temporal cognition.

Methods

1 · Similarity Judgment Task

We evaluate temporal cognition using a similarity judgment paradigm from cognitive science. For each pair of years drawn from 1525–2524, models are prompted to rate their similarity on a continuous 0–1 scale. With 1000 data points, this yields ~1 million pairwise judgments per model. A parallel number-to-number control replaces "year" with "number" to isolate temporal-specific processing.

Figure 2 · Similarity Matrices

Year-to-year (top row) and number-to-number (bottom row) similarity matrices across 12 models of varying scale. Larger models show a distinctive diagonal compression anchored near 2025, absent in the number control.

Distance Metrics

  • Log-Linear dlog(i,j) = |log(i) − log(j)|

    Weber-Fechner law without reference; predicts number cognition well.

  • Reference-Log-Linear dref(i,j) = |log|R−i| ○ log|R−j||

    Logarithmic distance from a subjective reference point R ≈ 2025; best predictor for larger models on the year task.

  • Levenshtein dlev(i,j)

    String-edit distance; captures surface orthographic similarity that emerges in deeper layers.

Models tested: 12 total — Qwen2.5 (1.5B → 72B), Llama 3 (1B → 70B), GPT-4o, Gemini-2.0-Flash. Temperature fixed at 0 for deterministic outputs.

2 · Neural Coding Analysis

For each year 1525–2524, we extract FFN neuron activations across all transformer layers at the last-token position, under both temporal ("Year: x-x-x-x") and numerical ("Number: x-x-x-x") input formats. Temporal-preferential neurons are identified by three joint criteria:

  • Effect size — Cohen's d > 2.0 between temporal and numerical conditions
  • Statistical significance — FDR-corrected p < 0.0001 (Benjamini-Hochberg)
  • Consistency — Preference for temporal condition in > 95% of years

The top 1,000 temporal-preferential neurons (by effect size) are then analyzed for their mean activation curve across years to assess logarithmic encoding.

3 · Representational Structure

Layer-by-layer linear probes (affine transformations f(h) = w·h + b) are trained to predict each of the three theoretical distances from hidden states at each layer. We use MSE loss, the Adam optimizer (lr = 1e-4), and evaluate via adjusted R² per layer. For large models, ~25 layers are sampled proportionally across network depth.

Figure 3 · Representational Probing

Layer-by-layer R² for each distance metric across the Qwen and Llama model families. Early layers favor dlog; deeper layers increasingly favor dref, with suppression of dlog in the Qwen series.

4 · Information Exposure Analysis

To probe whether temporal structure exists in the training corpus independent of model internals, we analyze year semantics using three independently pre-trained embedding models: Qwen3-Embedding-8B, text-embedding-3-large, and Gemini-embedding-001. Cosine similarity matrices are constructed for all year pairs; MDS is applied for visualization. Linear regression quantifies how well each theoretical distance predicts corpus-level semantic distance.

Results

Similarity Judgment Task

As models scale, a clear Weber-Fechner pattern emerges in the year-to-year task: pairs of years far from ~2025 are perceived as more similar than equidistant pairs near the reference. This pattern is absent in the number control. Quantitatively, the reference-log-linear distance achieves the highest R² in most models for the year task, while the log-linear distance best predicts the number task — consistent with prior work on numerical cognition in LLMs.

Non-parametric estimation of reference points via diagonal sliding window (window = 5) yields clear reference years: Llama-3.1-70B (2010), Gemini-2.0-Flash (2011), GPT-4o (2024), Qwen2.5-14B (2012), Qwen2.5-72B (2020). Models also consistently assign higher similarity to pairs of future years than equivalent past pairs.

Figure 4 · Reference Point Estimation

Non-parametric sliding window estimation of subjective reference points across models. Larger models show sharper, more clearly defined reference minima near the present.

Neural Coding Mechanism

Temporal-preferential neurons comprise 0.67–1.71% of total FFN neurons and concentrate in middle-to-late layers, indicating temporal cognition is a high-level abstract feature rather than early-layer processing. The mean activation of the top 1,000 temporal-preferential neurons forms a characteristic trough, bottoming at the reference year and rising logarithmically toward the past and future.

Layer-wise regression confirms logarithmic encoding strengthens with model scale: in Qwen2.5-72B, layer-71 neurons achieve R² = 0.756 for past years. A notable past-future asymmetry in neural coding contributes to the behavioral tendency of higher future-pair similarity.

Figure 5 · Temporal Neuron Activations

Mean activation curves of the top 1,000 temporal-preferential neurons across years 1525–2524. The trough bottoms near the model's subjective reference point and exhibits logarithmic flanks — more pronounced in larger models.

Representational Structure

Probing reveals a hierarchical construction from concrete to abstract:

  • Llama series: Smaller models (1B, 3B) primarily encode log-linear distance throughout. Larger models (8B, 70B) develop comparable reference-log-linear strength in final layers — both representations coexist.
  • Qwen series: A sequential pattern: dlog rises in early layers → dref rises in middle layers → dref peaks in later layers with concurrent suppression of dlog. Levenshtein distance becomes significant in later layers of several large models.

This trajectory confirms that temporal reference-orientation is a learned abstraction constructed progressively over network depth, not a surface-level pattern.

Information Exposure

All three embedding models reveal the same non-linear temporal structure in corpus semantics: dense clustering in the distant past and future, mirroring the behavioral pattern. Future years cluster tightly due to lower information richness — fewer distinct documented events — causing greater semantic overlap. This corpus-level structure provides the raw material for the model's emergent temporal asymmetry.

Quantitative regression confirms that reference-log-linear distance best explains corpus semantic distances, linking informational pre-structure directly to behavioral outcomes.

Figure 6 · Embedding Space Structure

MDS visualization of year semantic distances from three embedding models. Years cluster densely at the extremes and spread near the present, with future years showing notably high mutual similarity.

Discussion

The Experientialist Perspective

Our findings align with experientialist philosophy in cognitive science: cognitive patterns are irreducible phenomena where representational systems actively construct subjective models of the external world they are situated in. LLMs' temporal cognition is not simple data regurgitation nor human imitation — it is genuine subjective construction shaped by the interplay of architecture and informational environment.

This framing resolves the apparent paradox of human-like cognition in systems so architecturally different from biological brains. Both systems are exposed to overlapping information environments; convergent cognitive outcomes can emerge from this shared exposure even across radically different architectures.

Dimension Human LLM
Architecture Sparse, small-world, analog Dense, all-to-all, digital
Representation Fine-grained, contextually rich Statistical compression
Environment Continuous, embodied, multimodal Static text corpus snapshot
Learning Local synaptic, lifelong Global weight updates, fixed

Implications for AI Alignment

Behavioral alignment paradigms — RLHF, constitutional AI, red teaming — treat LLMs as black boxes to be constrained externally. As models scale, this paradigm faces a fundamental limitation: it cannot anticipate or shape the internal constructions that produce behavior.

The experientialist perspective suggests a different target: alignment must engage with the formative process by which a model's representational system builds its subjective world model. The goal shifts from making AI safe to making safe AI — systems whose emergent cognitive patterns are inherently aligned because their internal world-models are shaped accordingly.

Concretely, this requires:

  • Monitoring emergent representational and cognitive patterns during training
  • Understanding and intervening at the neuron → representation → behavior chain
  • Curating training environments (information exposures) to shape internal constructions

"The primary risk is not that LLMs become imperfect replicas of the human mind, but that they develop powerful, alien cognitive frameworks we cannot predict."

Conclusion

Through the similarity judgment task, we show that larger LLMs exhibit a profound multi-level convergence with human temporal cognition — not superficial mimicry, but emergence across three distinct levels:

Neuronal

Temporal-preferential neurons implement logarithmic coding convergent with biological neural systems.

Representational

Hierarchical construction from numerical values in shallow layers to reference-centered temporal orientation in deep layers.

Informational

The model internalizes a non-linear temporal structure pre-existing in its training corpus.

Collectively, these findings endorse an experientialist understanding of LLMs as active constructors of internal world-models rather than passive pattern matchers. AI alignment must evolve accordingly — beyond behavioral control to actively guiding the formation of a model's internal world from its source.
